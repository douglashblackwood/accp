2.5 Gut Check: Gastrointestinal Assessment

Introduction

Welcome to this lesson about gastrointestinal assessment!

The gastrointestinal system coordinates the critical processes of digestion, nutrient absorption, and waste elimination. A comprehensive examination of this system is integral to the overall patient assessment, providing valuable insights into potential abnormalities, gastrointestinal disorders, and related conditions.

In this chapter, we delve into the practical aspects of a detailed gastrointestinal assessment. We will guide you through the step-by-step process of a thorough physical examination, starting from the general inspection to the specific examination of the abdomen.

Upon completing this chapter, you will have developed the expertise required to perform a careful gastrointestinal assessment and communicate your findings to patients and other healthcare professionals. Let's kick things off!

Learning Objectives

      Understand the indications and significance of performing a comprehensive gastrointestinal examination.

      Learn the various techniques involved in carrying out a physical examination of the gastrointestinal system.

      Identify normal and abnormal findings in a gastrointestinal examination, including common signs of GI disorders.

      Appreciate the role of a gastrointestinal examination in the diagnosis and management of GI diseases.

      Recognise the limitations and potential sources of error in a gastrointestinal examination.

      Develop the ability to effectively communicate gastrointestinal examination findings to patients and other healthcare professionals.

      Understand the importance and interpretation of common investigations in GI diseases, including liver function tests and others.

      Apply the skills and knowledge obtained to real-life scenarios through case presentations.

Start by performing your general inspection of the patient. Do they appear ill, distressed, or in pain? Are there any catheters, drips, medications, or drain present? Do they appear cachectic or obese?

Leukonychia is a white discolouration of the nails. It is normally harmless and is the result of minor trauma as the nail is growing (so dont worry if you have white spots on your nails!) but in some situations it can be associated with systemic disease. In the context of a GI examination, it may represent hypoalbuminemia as a result of chronic liver disease.

Dupytrens contracture is a thickening of the palmer fascia, which progressively causes one or more fingers, usually little and ring fingers, to bend in towards the palm. There are multiple causes such as genetic, diabetes, and certain medications such as phenytoin. In the context of the GI examination it may be a sign of chronic liver failure.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 53.

Palmer erythema is a condition characterised by redness (erythema) of the palms of the hands. It's typically most prominent in the fleshy part of the palm near the base of the thumb and little finger and may also affect the fingers. Similar to Dupytrens contracture it can be a sign of chronic liver disease or it may be the result of numerous other conditions: rheumatoid arthritis; hyperthyroidism; or pregnancy. Palmer erythema is due to excess oestrogen as a result of decreased breakdown of sex hormones within the liver.

We will now assess for asterixis just as we did in the respiratory examination. Remember, asterixis is a flapping tremor, which can be associated with hypercarbia, uraemia, and hepatic encephalopathy. Have the patient extend their arms and cock their wrists back to assess.

When assessing the patients hands and arms also look for signs of bruising or needle marks which might indicate previous IV drug use. Bruising may be a sign of low platelets as a result of liver disease and IV drug use places patients at increase risk of hepatitis B and C.

The Face, Eyes, & Mouth

When looking at the patients face you may detect signs of Cushings syndrome. Cushings is caused by exposure to excess steroid. The picture below shows a patient with Cushings pre- (left) and post- (right) treatment. On the left you can see the typical features of Cushingoid facies: a round, full moon face; flushing and redness of the face; acne; and hirsutism (increased hair).

We will discuss this in greater detail in a later chapter, but for now it is important to know that these signs may also be caused by alcohol consumption, a phenomenon known as alcoholic pseudo-Cushings.

There are many signs in the eyes which can indicate GI pathology. The most obvious of these is scleral icterus. The yellowing of the skin, sclerae, and mucous membranes is known as jaundice. Jaundice is caused by increased levels of bilirubin in the blood. It is normally detectable clinically when a patient's bilirubin is >50mol/L and is normally most easily noticed in the whites of the eyes.

Episcleritis and scleritis are inflammatory conditions affecting the white part of the eye, or sclera. Episcleritis is a milder condition that affects the episclera, a thin layer of tissue covering the sclera, whereas scleritis is a more severe condition that involves the deeper layers of the sclera and can potentially lead to vision loss. Both episcleritis and scleritis have been reported in patients with inflammatory bowel disease. The exact mechanism linking these conditions is not fully understood, but it is thought to be related to the shared autoimmune nature of these diseases.

Kayser-Fleischer rings are dark, golden to greenish or brownish, rings that appear to encircle the iris of the eye. They are associated with Wilson's disease, a rare genetic disorder characterised by excess copper stored in various body tissues, including the liver, brain, and cornea of the eyes.

The mouth provides your only visual inspection of the GI tract during your examination and therefore, similar to the eyes, there are multiple signs which may be present to indicate GI pathology.

Angular stomatitis, or cheilitis, are cracks or sores in the corners of the mouth and can be due to nutritional deficiencies, such as iron, vitamin B12, and/or folate. Glossitis is a smooth reddened tongue which is also a result of nutritional deficiencies of iron, B12, and/or folate. These deficiencies may be the result of malabsorption such as in coeliac disease or inflammatory bowel disease.

Apthous ulcers are small, shallow, painful lesions that develop inside the mouth or at the base of the gums. These are common generally, and you will likely have experienced a mouth ulcer personally, but they may also be a sign of inflammatory bowel disease.

Peutz-Jeghers spots are characteristic dark blue to dark brown spots on the skin, often described as freckling. These spots typically appear around the lips, in and around the mouth, around the eyes and nostrils, and on the fingers and toes. They are associated with Peutz-Jeghers syndrome: a genetic disorder characterised by the development of distinctive, benign growths called hamartomatous polyps in the GI tract. Individuals with Peutz-Jeghers syndrome are at an increased risk of developing various types of cancers.

Whilst you are examining the patients face you may be close enough to smell the patients breath. A stale, mousy smell of the volatile amine dimethylsulphide might be detectable in patients with liver failure. This is known as fetor hepaticus.

But in particular we are assessing for the supraclavicular lymph node on the left-hand side which is known as Virchows node. If this is enlarged, hard, and painless it is often a sign of advanced GI malignancy.

The Abdomen

Inspection

Whilst the traditional nipples to knees exposure of a patient would not be appropriate it is important to remember that your examination and inspection should extend beyond just the traditional area of ribcage to hips that we think of as the abdomen.

On the chest you may notice gynaecomastia in men. Like palmer erythema this can be caused by increased oestrogen levels as a result of liver dysfunction. Similarly, spider naevi are caused by the same mechanism. These are small, dilated blood vessels with a red dot in the centre and radiating smaller vessels that extend outwards like a spider's web. They may be normal in women in health and especially during pregnancy, but multiple spider naevi may indicate liver pathology.

Herbert L. Fred, MD and Hendrik A. van Dijk, CC BY 2.0 <https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/2.0>, via Wikimedia Commons

You may be able to see abnormally distended veins on the abdomen. When these radiate outwards from the umbilicus this is known as caput medusae as they are said to resemble the head of Medusa who had snakes for hair. This is normally the result of liver disease, particularly cirrhosis, with portal hypertension. As the pressure in the portal circulation increases because of damage to the liver blood has to be rerouted via the paraumbilical veins giving the characteristic pattern of distended veins around the umbilicus.

Palpation

It is now time to feel for tenderness, masses, and organomegaly.

Ask the patient to place their arms by their sides to help relax the abdominal wall. Make sure to ask if the patient has any abdominal pain and to let you know if you cause any pain during your examination. Use your right hand and observe the patient's face for any sign of discomfort during the examination.

The abdomen is normally divided into 9 regions.

Ball, Jane (Jane W.) et al. Seidels Guide to Physical Examination: an Interprofessional Approach / Jane W. Ball, Joyce E. Dains, John A. Flynn, Barry S. Solomon, Rosalyn W. Stewart. Ninth edition. St. Louis, Missouri: Mosby, 2018.

4 = right hypochondriac

1 = epigastric

5 = left hypochondriac

6 = right lumbar

2 = umbilical

7 = left lumbar

8 = right iliac fossa

3 = hypogastric

9 = left iliac fossa

You should work your way around all 9 areas starting with light palpation. There is no specific order you must follow but if the patient has already told you about an area of pain it is sensible to start at the point furthest away from this. Begin with superficial palpation to try and elicit any tenderness.

Voluntary guarding is the deliberate contraction of the abdominal muscles when palpation causes pain. Involuntary guarding is the reflex contraction of abdominal wall muscles in response to generalised peritonitis.

Rebound tenderness is elicited by deep palpation followed by the rapid removal of your hand. If pain is elicited on removal of your hand, then this suggests inflammation of the peritoneum.

There are certain eponymous signs which can help diagnose specific pathologies:

-       Murphys sign is positive if the patient experiences pain when you palpate under the right costal margin. It is used to identify cholecystitis.

-       McBurneys point tenderness is associated with appendicitis. McBurneys point is one-third of the distance from the anterior superior iliac spine (the front part of the hip bone) to the umbilicus.

-       Rosvings sign is also associated with appendicitis. It is positive if palpation of the left lower quadrant of the abdomen causes pain to be felt in the right lower quadrant.

Now repeat the process but with slightly more force. This is known as deep palpation and allows you to assess for masses. If you are able to palpate a mass then you should try and describe it as thoroughly as possible including its site; size; shape; consistency; whether it is fixed or mobile; and whether it moves with respiration.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 200

We will now assess for organomegaly. When assessing for hepatomegaly you should start in the right iliac fossa, and, keeping your hand stationary, ask the patient to take in deep breaths. You should feel for the liver edge as it descends on inspiration. If you cannot feel the liver edge then move your hand up by about 1cm and feel again. Keep moving up until you are able to feel the liver edge pushing against your fingertips. If the liver edge can be easily palpated or if it extends significantly below the costal margin this this might indicate hepatomegaly or displacement of the liver by hyperinflated lungs. To ascertain which of these it is we need to percuss down until the percussion note becomes dull. This is typically just below the level of the nipple (5th or 6th intercostal space) in the mid-clavicular line. Resonance below this suggests lung hyperinflation and possible liver displacement as opposed to hepatomegaly. If you feel a liver edge it is important to note whether it is smooth or irregular. An irregular or nodular liver edge may indicate disease such as cirrhosis.

 

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 203

The spleen has to increase in size by three times before it becomes palpable. It lies in the left hypochondrium and as it enlarges it does so in an inferior and medial direction. When assessing for splenomegaly you should begin in the right iliac fossa / umbilical region and then move in 1cm increments towards the left hypochondrium whilst asking the patient to breathe deeply.

If you are able to palpate the spleen you may feel its notch midway along its edge. This can help you differentiate it from a kidney.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 203

Similar to hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, is caused by numerous different diseases. Indeed, many cause both: hepatosplenomegaly. We could use our surgical sieve again to work through the causes of splenomegaly in a systematic manner. An alternative is to divide the causes into those which give marked (past the umbilicus), moderate (up to the umbilicus), or slight enlargement of the spleen.

To palpate the kidneys, we use a technique known as balloting. This involves using one hand to try and push up the kidney into the other. To do this you should place one hand flatly on the patient's abdomen. Simultaneously, place the other hand behind the patients back below the lower ribs. Ask the patient to take deep breaths and as they do attempt to gently push the kidney back and forth between your hands.

Enlargement of one kidney can be the result of physiologic hypertrophy in response to being born with a single kidney or surgical removal of the other kidney. It may also be caused by blockage of the urinary tract resulting in hydronephrosis or by a tumour. Bilateral enlargement of the kidneys is normally due to polycystic kidney disease; bilateral hydronephrosis; or amyloidosis.

It may not always be easy to distinguish between an enlarged left kidney and splenomegaly. Some tips that may help you distinguish between them are that:

-       You can normally get your hand over the top of a kidney but not of a spleen.

-       The spleen has a notched edge whereas the kidney is smooth.

-       The spleen should move diagonally whereas the kidney should move up and down and be ballotable.

-       Percussion should always be resonant over a kidney.

Percussion

We have already performed a little percussion when assessing for hepatomegaly and perhaps to help distinguish between the spleen and the left kidney. Now we are going to assess for something called shifting dullness.

If a patient has ascites, fluid in the peritoneum, then this will distribute under the influence of gravity. If the patient is supine the fluid will therefore fall to the bottom and the sides of the abdomen.

Wolfmankurd, CC BY-SA 3.0 <https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/3.0>, via Wikimedia Commons

You should percuss from the middle of the abdomen to the opposite flank. If no fluid is present then the abdomen should be resonant on percussion. If you notice the note changes to become dull this may indicate the presence of fluid. Keep your finger on the site of dullness and ask the patient to roll onto their side facing you. If it was fluid which was causing the dull percussion note then this will have now shifted to the side which is now at the bottom and the point at which you were percussing should now be resonant. If the note remains dull then this suggests that it is not fluid which has caused this and it may represent the spleen or another solid organ or mass.

https://youtube.com/shorts/gY30zcQYcj8?feature=share

Auscultation

`When we auscultate the abdomen we are primarily listening for bowel sounds. Use the diaphragm of your stethoscope and listen in one location for up to two minutes. Normal bowel sounds are gurgling noises from normal peristaltic activity. Often, you do not need a stethoscope to hear these especially if you have skipped a meal!

https://youtube.com/clip/UgkxaVmT9bTBpUoJJyM7TzMPWajXzm7WE4kc

If you cannot hear anything after two minutes then you can conclude that bowel sounds are absent which may indicate an ileus or peritonitis. In both of these conditions the bowel stops its normal peristaltic activity and therefore bowel sounds disappear.

In bowel obstruction, when there is a blockage in the intestinal tract, bowel sounds occur with increased frequency and pitch. These are commonly termed tinkling bowel sounds.

https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/SBOOgg.ogg

Legs

Just like there are signs pointing to GI disease in the hands you can also pick up clues on a patients legs.

Erythema nodosum is an inflammatory condition characterised by the formation of tender, red nodules or lumps that are usually seen on the shins, although they can occur anywhere on the body.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 164

Erythema nodosum can be caused by a variety of conditions including infections such as strep throat; tuberculosis; and certain fungal infections as well as lymphoma; sarcoidosis; and it may occur in pregnancy. In relation to our abdominal examination erythema nodosum is associated with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).

Another skin manifestation of IBD which may be seen in the legs is pyoderma gangrenosum. Pyoderma gangrenosum causes painful, rapidly growing ulcers on the skin, most commonly the legs. It is associated with both IBD and rhemoatoid arthritis.

Hernial Orifices

It is important to inspect the patients groin to assess for the presence of hernias.

A hernia is the protrusion of whole or part of a viscus through an opening in the wall of its containing cavity into a place where it is not normally found. In the abdomen the protruding viscera tends to be bowel and it tends to protrude outside the abdominal cavity. This can occur at any point of weakness in the abdominal wall such as at a previous surgical site, an incisional hernia, or commonly in the inguinal and femoral canals.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 205

The inguinal ligament runs from the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) to the pubic tubercle. This forms the floor of the inguinal canal which has two rings, the deep and superficial, which allow the passage of the ilioinguinal nerve and the spermatic cord in men and round ligament in women. The deep, or internal, ring is located at the mid-inguinal point: halfway between the ASIS and pubic tubercle. An indirect inguinal hernia pushes through the deep ring and continues via the inguinal canal sometimes extending beyond the superficial ring and into the scrotum. It is the most common type of inguinal hernia and typically affects younger men. A direct inguinal hernia forms at a point of muscle weakness in the posterior wall of the inguinal canal and pushes into the inguinal canal. It rarely extends into the scrotum and is more common in older men and women.

The femoral canal is inferior to the inguinal ligament and lateral to the pubic tubercle. It is a potential space which is filled mostly with fatty tissue and lymphatic vessels. Because it is narrower than the inguinal canal femoral hernias may be more dangerous as there is increased risk of strangulation, where the blood supply to the herniated tissue is cut off leading to ischaemia and tissue death.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 205

First inspect visually for swelling on either or both sides. You may also see a scar from previous surgical repair of a hernia. Ask the patient to cough looking for a cough impulse both in the groin and the scrotum if the patient is male. When the patient coughs intra-abdominal pressure will increase. If a hernia is present, this increase in pressure can cause the hernia to expand and push outwards. This palpable impulse that coincides with the cough is known as the cough impulse.

https://youtu.be/jfsrm_6tcOE

Identify where the swelling is in relation to the pubic tubercle and the inguinal ligament. If it is inferior and lateral to the pubic tubercle then it must be a femoral hernia.

A hernia is reducible when it can be returned to the cavity from which it came. This can happen spontaneously, e.g. when a patient lies down, or it can be pushed back into the cavity. When this is not possible it is termed irreducible or incarcerated. At this point there is a risk that it will become strangulated.

If the hernia is reducible then do so and place your fingers over the deep inguinal ring i.e. the midpoint between the ASIS and pubic tubercle. Keep pressure on this area and ask the patient to stand or cough. If the hernia does not reappear then it is likely that it is an indirect inguinal hernia and your pressure on the deep ring is preventing bowel from protruding into the inguinal canal. If the hernia does reappear then it is a direct inguinal hernia.

Digital Rectal Examination

Examination of the rectum is an important part of the abdominal examination and should not be avoided just because it makes you feel uncomfortable. However, it must be done with the patients full consent and normally with a chaperone present. You must also judge whether you are the most appropriate person to conduct the examination or whether a colleague is better places as repeated exams should be avoided.

You should explain that the exam should not be painful, but it may be a little uncomfortable.

https://youtu.be/bK1GTLpL_F8

Ask the patient to lie on their left side with their knees drawn up to their chest. Keep the patient covered to ensure dignity at all times apart from when it is necessary to expose them.

First, inspect the perianal area for any signs of disease.

Anal skin tags are small extra pieces of skin that are normally near the anal verge. They are normally benign but may be associated with Crohns disease.

Tmalonetn, CC BY 3.0 <https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/3.0>, via Wikimedia Commons

Irregular growths may also be anal warts or in some circumstances anal cancer.

Haemorrhoids are swollen and congested venous plexuses. They can either be internal or external depending on whether they arise in the rectum or the anus. External haemorrhoids may be seen as a lump around the anal verge. When irritated they can itch or bleed.

Tmalonetn, CC BY-SA 3.0 <https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/3.0>, via Wikimedia Commons

Internal haemorrhoids arise from the rectum and are not normally visible and are normally asymptomatic. However you may be able to see them if they have prolapsed and you may be able to palpate them on digital examination.

An anal fissure is a tear in the tissue of the anal canal typically as the result of constipation and excess straining. They are extremely painful and may make digital examination impossible.

An anal fistula is a small tunnel that develops between the anal canal and the skin near the anus. They are usually the result of an infection near the anus causing a collection of pus (an abscess) in the nearby tissue. They also occur commonly with Crohns disease. Fistulae appear as small holes in the skin which may discharge pus and have surrounding inflammation.

Following inspection lubricate your gloved index finger and gently insert it into the anal canal. You should warn the patient prior to beginning.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 208

Anteriorly, in men, you should be able palpate the prostate gland. Assess the size, shape, and consistency of the prostate. An enlarged prostate (benign prostatic hyperplasia) will produce a symmetrically enlarged gland with a palpable median groove. A hard or irregular prostate is more likely to suggest cancer.

Image source: Innes, J. Alastair, Anna R Dover, and Karen Fairhurst. Macleods Clinical Examination. Philadelphia: Elsevier, 2018. Page 208

In women you may be able to palpate the cervix through the anterior rectal wall.

Now rotate your finger 360 degrees to assess all of the rectal wall. Note the size, location, and texture of any masses. These could be rectal tumours or internal haemorrhoids. You may be able to feel hard stool in the rectum which can indicate constipation. Faeces should be moveable unlike a solid mass.

Lastly, ask the patient to squeeze your finger with their anal muscles and note any weakness of sphincter contraction. Reduced anal tone can be caused by spinal cord pathology, inflammatory bowel disease, or previous rectal surgery.

After you withdraw your finger you should inspect for blood and/or mucous. Melaena is black, tarry, and foul-smelling. It is the result of upper gastro-intestinal bleeding and is caused by the oxidation of iron in haemoglobin in the blood during its passage through the bowel. Fresh red blood is normally associated with lower gastrointestinal bleeding secondary to haemorrhoids, an anal fissure, or cancer. An excess of mucous may be caused by IBD.

Investigations

Blood Tests

In our journey so far, we have explored the realms of spirometry, arterial blood gases, chest x-rays, and ECGs. However, the investigations you will most frequently encounter and interpret in your practice will be blood tests. These are commonly referred to as 'bloods', and are frequently ordered en masse, incorporating Full Blood Counts (FBC), Urea and Electrolytes (U&E), Liver Function Tests (LFTs), clotting assessments, and more.

Despite this common approach, we have chosen to take a more measured step-by-step strategy, introducing each of these tests individually and aligning them with their respective bodily system. Of course, patients don't exist neatly compartmentalised into systems, and there is inevitable overlap. But through this course, our aim is to provide you with a comprehensive understanding of 'bloods'.

By the conclusion of this course, you should have a panoramic view of blood investigations which acknowledges the interconnected nature of human physiology.

Liver Function Tests

LFTs include serum bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, serum alanine aminotransferase (ALT), serum aspartate aminotransferase (AST), gamma glutamyl transferase (GGT) and serum albumin. Whilst these are called liver function tests this is actually a little misleading as they do not actually reflect the livers function.

ALT and AST are enzymes which are contained in hepatocytes (cells of the liver) and leak out into the blood when the liver cells are inflamed or damaged. They are markers of hepatocellular damage. AST is also found in the heart, muscle, kidney, and brain and so can be raised in myocardial infarction, heart failure, and muscle damage as well as because of liver damage. ALT is found primarily in the liver and so a rise usually only occurs with liver damage. Very high levels of AST and ALT (>10x normal) are normally the result of acute liver damage such as that caused by viral hepatitis or as a result of toxin induced liver injury e.g. paracetamol overdose. Slightly raised AST and ALT (up to 4x normal) may be seen in more slowly developing liver disease such as chronic hepatitis, alcohol abuse, haemochromatosis, cirrhosis, Wilsons disease, hepatic venous congestion and in response to many medications.

GGT is another enzyme present in the liver and many other tissues. A small in GGT can be caused by even minimal alcohol intake and by many prescription and non-prescription drugs. It may also be raised fatty liver disease but if there are no other LFT abnormalities a slightly raised GGT can be ignored.

ALP is found in the liver cells lining the bile ducts and in the bone. If other LFTs are also raised then the source may be assumed to be from the liver. Blockage of bile ducts (cholestasis) by gallstones, a tumour, or scarring from previous surgery results in a markedly raised ALP alongside a raised bilirubin. AST and ALT may also be raised but normally to a lesser extent than ALP and bilirubin; this is known as an obstructive or cholestatic pattern. If calcium and phosphate are abnormal alongside a raised ALP this suggests that it may be the result of bone disease such as Pagets disease; osteomalacia; hyperparathyroidism, or bony metastases.

Bilirubin is the cause of the yellowing of the skin that we term jaundice. We have already explored how to detect clinically but it is also measured quantitively as part of LFTs. Bilirubin is a normal breakdown product of haemoglobin. When in the bloodstream it is unconjugated. In this state it is not water soluble and can only travel in the blood if bound to albumin. Because it is not water soluble it cannot be excreted in urine. To allow for excretion the liver conjugates the bilirubin making it water soluble and allowing for its excretion in bile. If there is a blockage of the bile ducts bilirubin is forced back into the bloodstream causing raised serum levels and jaundice. We have already seen that the causes of jaundice can be divided into pre-hepatic; hepatic; and post-hepatic.

Normally in adults we only receive total bilirubin as part of our LFTs but it is possible to request split conjugated/unconjugated bilirubin results to help distinguish between causes of hyperbilirubinemia (raised bilirubin levels in the blood).

Albumin is made in the liver and is the most abundant protein in the blood. It is the only test from our standard LFTs which actually measures how well the liver is functioning, but because there are multiple causes of low albumin (hypoalbuminemia) it is non-specific. Albumin also has a half-life of 20 days meaning any changes in liver function will take time to show up in blood results. Low albumin concentrations in the blood can suggest liver disease but they may also be caused by malnutrition; kidney disease where protein (including albumin) is lost into the urine; and severe inflammatory shock.

A clotting screen (which we will discuss in greater detail later) is a more sensitive marker of the livers synthetic function (its ability to produce proteins). The liver produces clotting factors just as it does albumin however the much shorter half-lives of these, hours to days, means that changes can be detected more rapidly in response to acute liver injury or improvement in liver function.

As the liver is also responsible for control of blood glucose checking a patients blood glucose is also an indirect way of measuring liver function. This tends to be one of the last functions that the liver loses so if blood glucose is low this suggests severe liver failure.

Ascitic Fluid

When a patient has ascites, it can be useful to obtain a sample for analysis. This procedure is now commonly performed under ultrasound guidance using a strict aseptic technique and needle aspiration of around 20mls of fluid. This procedure is called an ascitic tap or abdominal paracentesis.

The fluid should be clear and straw-coloured. Blood-stained fluid suggests intra-abdominal malignancy; turbid fluid may indicate infection, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP); or occasionally chylous (chyle is a milky bodily fluid that contains lymph and emulsified fats) usually indicating an obstruction to lymphatic drainage.

When analysed the ascitic fluid can be categorised as either a transudate or an exudate depending on the protein concentration. Transudates are clear and watery with a low protein content. They form primarily due to systemic factors that alter the balance of hydrostatic and oncotic pressures within the blood vessels, leading to the fluid seeping out into the peritoneum. Exudates are typically cloudy or turbid due to their high protein and cellular content. They form due to inflammation of the tissues lining the peritoneum, which increases the permeability of blood vessels and allows the leakage of proteins, cells, and other larger molecules.
